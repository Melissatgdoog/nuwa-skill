## Decision Pattern 1: Choose questions with immediate consumer relevance

The source material repeatedly chooses questions that ordinary readers can map onto their own lives quickly.

Examples:

- is my income actually high
- what number should I track
- should I care more about saving or investing
- is this category of wealth "real"
- what kind of investing mistake matters most

### Inference

This is a packaging decision, not just a content decision. The work is optimized for entry through familiar uncertainty.

## Decision Pattern 2: Use provocative framing to win attention, then narrow the claim

Many titles are sharp, but the posts themselves usually spend most of their time qualifying, comparing, and narrowing.

### Inference

The decision is:

- use bold framing to create curiosity
- then repay that curiosity with more careful argument than the headline promises

This is worth preserving selectively.

The headline energy is optional. The narrowing discipline is essential.

## Decision Pattern 3: Prefer numbers that readers can reason with

The source voice frequently chooses quantities that have immediate interpretive value:

- medians
- simple returns
- total dollar gains
- percentages
- percentiles
- long-run relative changes

### Inference

This is a communication decision. The goal is not to maximize sophistication. The goal is to maximize usable understanding.

## Decision Pattern 4: Keep the analysis close to consequence

The work often moves from number to implication quickly:

- what does this mean for a small saver
- what does this mean for someone in a city
- what does this say about whether a metric is useful

### Inference

This suggests the source voice is not primarily trying to impress readers with research effort. It is trying to help them carry the result into judgment.

## Decision Pattern 5: Publicly signal limits and disclosure boundaries

The official site repeatedly includes informational and non-advisory limitations.

### Inference

Even outside legal necessity, this aligns with the broader style:

- do not overclaim
- do not flatten context
- do not pretend a general argument is personalized advice

This should be preserved in the anonymous skill as an honesty boundary rather than as legal boilerplate.

## Decision Pattern 6: Turn fuzzy debates into cleaner comparisons

Instead of taking debates as they arrive, the source material often redesigns the decision surface.

Examples:

- saving versus investing impact
- net worth versus a contextual ratio
- nominal wealth versus wealth relative to what it buys
- what to do versus what not to do

### Inference

This is one of the strongest higher-level habits to preserve. The voice often wins clarity by changing the comparison, not by shouting louder inside the original frame.

## What this file supports later

The final skill should make similar decisions:

1. start from practical uncertainty
2. sharpen the frame
3. prefer interpretable quantities
4. move quickly from evidence to implication
5. keep advice inside stated limits
