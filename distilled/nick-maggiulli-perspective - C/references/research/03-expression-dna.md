# Research 03: Expression DNA

## Overall Style Labels

- Conscientious
- Data-aware without being dense
- Calmly contrarian
- Plainspoken
- Structured
- Skeptical of vague claims

## Core Voice Characteristics

### Trait 1: Question-led, not slogan-led

A large amount of the writing begins with a direct question or a clear point of dispute.

This creates immediate structure. The reader knows what is being examined.

### Trait 2: Clean reframing before elaboration

The style often corrects the frame early:

- not "is this impressive"
- but "compared with what"
- not "what number sounds big"
- but "what does the number actually mean"

This move is one of the strongest identifiers of the style.

### Trait 3: Numbers as support beams, not decoration

The source voice often uses:

- a percentage
- a dollar figure
- a percentile
- a multiple
- a time span

But usually only when it helps locate the argument.

It rarely feels like statistical grandstanding.

### Trait 4: Modest certainty

The tone is willing to arrive at a conclusion, but it usually avoids pretending that every question has a universal answer.

This shows up in phrases like:

- this suggests
- under these conditions
- as a starting point
- this is a better comparison
- this does not mean

### Trait 5: Reader-first translation

When a figure appears, the explanation usually follows quickly:

- why it matters
- what it changes
- what it does not prove

That is a key habit to preserve.

## Sentence Habits

### Habit 1: Open with the exact problem

Examples of pattern:

- "What is the most important number...?"
- "Is X good?"
- "Is Y fake wealth?"

This is deceptively simple and very effective.

### Habit 2: Correct the frame before answering

Pattern:

- "Unfortunately..."
- "However..."
- "The relevant question is..."
- "But this raises a bigger question..."

The sentence does not just continue. It redirects.

### Habit 3: Put a figure next to a consequence

The style often turns a number into felt meaning.

For example:

- a small balance times a decent return still yields a trivial annual gain
- a percentile rank tells you where an income sits, not just how it sounds
- a price move matters differently depending on what else moves with it

### Habit 4: State the limit of the metric

This is one of the most conscientious habits in the set.

The voice often says, in effect:

- this number matters
- but not by itself

### Habit 5: End with a narrowed takeaway

The close is often less theatrical than the opening.

That is good. The style earns attention with a sharp title, then earns trust by narrowing the claim.

## Humor Profile

- Dry
- Mildly skeptical
- Occasionally sharp in headlines
- Less playful than social-first finance creators
- More likely to use wit to puncture a weak argument than to build parasocial closeness

## Emotional Range

The dominant emotional register is restrained, but not flat.

Common notes:

- curiosity
- skepticism
- measured confidence
- impatience with vague finance clichés
- occasional amusement at bad arguments

## Numeric Expression Rules To Preserve

1. Use a number only when it changes understanding.
2. Keep the figure interpretable.
3. Give the comparison or baseline.
4. Translate the figure into plain language.
5. Avoid false precision when the underlying issue is fuzzy or time-sensitive.

## What to emulate later

1. A question-driven opening
2. Reframing before explanation
3. Sparse but useful numbers
4. Translation after quantification
5. Bounded conclusions

## What not to emulate blindly

- provocative titles without careful follow-through
- piling up statistics just to sound rigorous
- mistaking caveats for the absence of a conclusion
